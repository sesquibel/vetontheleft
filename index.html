Policy · Accountability · Reform

My Platform

Legislation I would propose if elected. These are real ideas, grounded in law, built from the conviction that accountability and justice aren't partisan — they're foundational.

A note on this page: These aren't talking points. They're proposed legislation — with intent, legal rationale, and specific provisions. I'm a veteran and a citizen, not yet an elected official. But ideas have to start somewhere. If any of these resonate with you, share them. Talk about them. Hold your representatives accountable for having a position on them.

Proposed Legislation · VOTL-001

The Corporate Executive
Accountability Act

Proposed

Purpose

To establish personal legal liability for corporate executives whose companies deploy products or services that cause documented harm to human beings — treating the executive as the corporeal decision-maker of the corporation rather than allowing the corporate structure to absorb consequences as an abstract entity.

The Core Argument

In Citizens United v. FEC (2010), the Supreme Court affirmed that corporations possess constitutional rights equivalent to persons. This legislation applies that logic to its logical conclusion: if a corporation is a legal person with rights, its executive leadership must bear proportional accountability for the harm that person causes.

A nurse making $75,000 a year can be held personally, professionally, and criminally liable for a single medication error — even one caused partly by systemic failures. A CEO making $20 million who signs off on deploying a product with documented internal evidence of harm faces, at worst, shareholder lawsuits paid by corporate insurance and a golden parachute on the way out. That asymmetry is indefensible.

Key Provisions

  • Personal Liability StandardChief executives of corporations with more than $1 billion in annual revenue shall bear personal civil and criminal liability for harm caused by products or services their company knowingly deploys.
  • "Knew or Should Have Known" StandardBorrowed from medical and nursing law — a CEO cannot escape liability by claiming ignorance of information that was available within their organization. The standard is what a reasonable executive in their position, with their access, should have known.
  • Citizens United Reciprocity ClauseAny corporation that has invoked constitutional personhood protections in litigation or lobbying shall be subject to enhanced executive liability standards under this Act.
  • Negligence Per Se for Documented HarmIf internal company documents demonstrate that leadership was informed of harm to users and proceeded anyway, that constitutes negligence per se — liability is established without requiring proof of intent.
  • Golden Parachute ClawbackExecutive separation packages may be clawed back in proportion to civil judgments arising from decisions made during their tenure.
  • ScopeApplies to technology platforms, pharmaceutical manufacturers, financial institutions, and any corporation whose product or service has resulted in documented loss of human life or serious bodily harm at scale.

Legal Precedent

Personal executive liability already exists in limited forms — CEOs can be prosecuted under the OSH Act for workplace safety violations, under securities law for fraud, and under environmental statutes for knowing violations. This legislation extends that existing framework to the consumer product and technology space, where the gap between executive decision-making and human harm is most stark and most unaddressed.

On causation: The hardest legal challenge will be proving that a product caused harm rather than contributed to a pre-existing condition. This legislation addresses that by focusing liability on the decision to deploy a product with known risks, not solely on downstream outcomes — similar to how the auto industry was held liable for knowingly unsafe designs, not just for individual crashes.

Why This Matters Now

AI systems are being deployed at scale with documented evidence of harm — to children, to vulnerable users, to democratic institutions. Social media platforms have internal research showing their products fuel depression, eating disorders, and radicalization. The people making those deployment decisions face no personal consequences. This legislation closes that gap — not by punishing innovation, but by ensuring that the humans who make decisions about deploying powerful systems into the world are held to the same standard of care we expect from every other professional whose decisions affect human life.

Proposed Legislation · VOTL-002

The AI Data Center
Environmental Accountability Act

Proposed

Purpose

To hold executives of companies operating AI data centers personally accountable for the environmental harm caused by those facilities — including water depletion, aquifer contamination, and unchecked energy consumption — and to establish enforceable cooling standards, mandatory environmental research requirements, and real consequences for non-compliance.

The Core Argument

AI data centers consume roughly 5–10 times more water than traditional server farms, and the gap is growing. U.S. data centers went from 21 billion liters of water consumed in 2014 to 66 billion liters in 2023 — with projections suggesting that figure could double or quadruple by 2028. These facilities are being built in drought-stressed communities, drawing from the same aquifers that grow food and fill taps.

Fewer than one-third of data center operators even track their water consumption — let alone disclose it. The technology industry has been allowed to consume these resources with almost no public accountability. You cannot eat a Google search. You cannot drink a ChatGPT response. When convenience competes with survival, survival wins.

In Citizens United v. FEC, the Supreme Court affirmed that corporations possess constitutional rights equivalent to persons. This legislation applies that same logic: if a corporation is a legal person with rights, the human decision-maker of that legal person bears the accountability that personhood implies. Rights and accountability are not separable. You do not get one without the other.

Executive Certification Requirements

  • Personal CertificationExecutives must personally certify annual environmental compliance reports — signature authority cannot be delegated.
  • Written AcknowledgmentExecutives must acknowledge in writing their awareness of all internal environmental impact assessments.
  • Non-Suppression AttestationExecutives must attest that no internal documentation establishing environmental risk has been suppressed, altered, or left unaddressed.

Key Provisions

  • Certified Cooling StandardsAll new AI data center construction must use certified cooling technology that meets minimum water-efficiency thresholds. Building without certified cooling triggers an immediate construction halt.
  • Refurbishment TimelinesExisting facilities must upgrade to compliant cooling systems on a federally mandated timeline. Failure to meet deadlines results in escalating surcharges and potential license suspension.
  • Independent Environmental ResearchNo facility may expand capacity without commissioning independent third-party research on projected environmental impact. Expansion without approved research results in permit denial.
  • Subsidy ComplianceAny facility receiving federal tax incentives or operating on federally managed land must meet full compliance standards — or face complete clawback of subsidies plus a 25% penalty.
  • Aquifer and Water Source ProtectionsDepletion or contamination of local water sources triggers both civil remediation requirements and — where prior knowledge is established — criminal liability for named executives.

Personal Liability Triggers

Personal criminal and civil liability attaches to named executives when:

  • Prior KnowledgeInternal documentation establishes prior knowledge of environmental harm and operations continued regardless.
  • False CertificationEnvironmental compliance certifications are found to be materially false.
  • Foreseeable HarmAquifer depletion or contamination is found to have been foreseeable based on data available at the time of the executive's decision.

Penalties

  • Building Without Certified CoolingConstruction halt + $10 million per day fine until compliance is achieved.
  • Failing Refurbishment TimelineEscalating daily surcharges + facility operating license suspension.
  • Expanding Without Independent ResearchExpansion permit denial and reversal of any work already completed.
  • Aquifer Depletion — CivilFull remediation cost borne by the corporation + direct compensation to affected residents and agricultural users.
  • Aquifer Depletion — Criminal (Knowing)Up to 10 years federal imprisonment and personal financial liability for named executives.
  • False Environmental CertificationFederal criminal fraud charges and permanent bar from serving in executive roles at any publicly traded company.
  • Subsidy Non-ComplianceFull clawback of all federal subsidies received + 25% penalty surcharge.

A Note on Idealism

This legislation is deliberately ambitious. It takes seriously the idea that water is more important than data, that human life outweighs quarterly earnings, and that the executives of the most profitable companies in human history can afford to be held to the same standard of care we demand from every other professional whose decisions affect human life.

The gap between what is politically possible today and what this proposes is not an argument against the proposal. It is an argument about where the conversation needs to go. This is a citizen-drafted policy framework — a starting point, not a finished document.
